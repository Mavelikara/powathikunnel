About the Powathikunnel Family

A heritage spanning nearly two millennia — from the first Christian converts of Kerala to 773 documented members across 12 generations, settled across India and the world.

📜 The Full Family History

The complete narrative as recorded in the Powathikunnel Kudumbayogam family history book. Each chapter can be expanded for the full account.

Origins & The First Christians of Kerala
AD 52 — St. Thomas · Pakalomattom Family · Kuravilangadu
▸

It is believed that St. Thomas reached Kerala in AD 52. He performed miracles among the Brahmins near Palayoor, Chavakkad, and many converted to Christianity — among them the renowned Pakalomattom, Sankarapuri, Kali, and Kalikavu families. St. Thomas entrusted the Pakalomattom family to serve as priests and Archdeacons of the new Christian community.

After ninety-three years, some of these families moved to Kodungallore, settling at the North Market (Vadakkeangadi). The Syrian Christians who arrived with Knaithomman settled at the South Market. The Pakalomattom Archdeacons continued to lead the Malankara Nasrani community, with their headquarters remaining at Kodungallore until AD 1559.

In time, many Pakalomattom families moved to Kuravilangadu, near Ettumannoore, where they joined families from Sankarapuri, Kali, and Kalikavu. Together, they constructed the Kuravilangadu Church. At Kuravilangadu, the families divided into two branches: Vadakkedam (Northerners) and Thekkedam (Southerners). The Thekkedathu families later moved to Athirampuzha for business, and the Powathikunnel family traces its origin from the Vadakkedathu branch that remained at Athirampuzha.

The Vadakkedathu families at Athirampuzha — Pootham, Makkii, Edam, Thadam, Vadakkedam, Mathankunnu — jointly constructed the Athirampuzha Church in AD 800, with principal funding from the ladies known as Thekkedathu Muthy and Kaipuzha Powathil Muthy. In AD 1030, the Thekkedathu family moved to Mannanom, three miles north of Adichira on the Kottayam–Ettumannoore road.

The Five Brothers & The Founding Branches
AD 1800 — Mannanom · Kottayam · Kattachira · Changanachery
▸

During AD 1800, a grave incident occurred at the Thekkedathu family home in Mannanom. Five brothers jointly killed a servant of the Vadakkumkoore King who had attempted to molest their sister. To escape the king's death penalty, all five brothers fled to the territory of the Thekkumkoore Ruler.

Each brother settled in a different place, founding the branches that survive today:

  • One brother settled at Kottayam with a Vadakkedathu family member already running a coconut oil business at Palathunkal — now known as the Palathunkal Family.
  • One brother, Itty Avirah, settled at Kattachira, near Kottayam — he is the ancestor of the Ulahannan line of the Mavelikara family.
  • One brother settled at Thuruthy Rappuzha with the family name Ittithanathu — founding the Chalackkal, Kayyalakam, Erumachadom, and related families.
  • One brother remained at Thuruthy itself, taking the family name Alanchery.
  • The fifth brother moved to Changanachery and started a business — he is the direct ancestor of the Powathikunnel Mallappally family.
Powathikunnel at Mallappally
AD 1719 — Varghese Vazhappallil · Poovana Panicker · Bhagavathikunnu
▸

Mr. Varghese Vazhappallil, descended from the Changanachery brother of the Thekkedathu family, moved to Mallappally in AD 1719 at the personal invitation of the local ruler, Poovana Panicker. He was appointed as the Nasrani — responsible for refining the temple offerings such as milk and ghee according to Hindu custom — and also established a business at the Mallappally market.

The ruler gave him a residential place called Bhagavathikunnu. The name Powathikunnel is understood to have been derived from Bhagavathikunnel.

The Powathikunnel family flourished at Mallappally, and today more than 400 families are present at Mallappally and its surrounding areas. The main family branches of the Powathikunnel Kudumbayogam are: Powathikunnel, Palackkamannil, Payyampallil, Vattasseril, Thuruthyvallil, and Vazhayil.

The famous Orthodox Church Metropolitan H.H. Geevarghese Mar Deevannasios belonged to the Powathikunnel Vattasseril family — widely known as Vattasseril Thirumeni. The Powathikunnel Kudumbayogam published the family history book, including a second edition.

Varghese Vazhappallil is considered the first-generation forefather of the entire Powathikunnel family at Mallappally, Mavelikara, and Edathua.

The Mavelikara Branch — Mallappally Varghese
AD 1806 · 4th Generation · Founder of the Mavelikara Powathikunnel Family
▸

Mr. Varghese (known as Mallappally Varghese), son of the third-generation Tommy Powathikunnel of Mallappally, moved to Mavelikara along with his four sons, taking the house name Powathikunnel. He brought with him his share of the family property proceeds. His wife was from the Kulathakkal family, Mavelikara, and his mother was Ms. Mariamma, sister of Palakkunnathu Malpan.

They arrived on a Sunday and, after attending church, searched for accommodation. They stayed for two days with Mr. Pilamoodan of Pilamoottil House, Nadakkavu, before moving to Nadupurackal House, Praikkara.

Mallappally Varghese purchased the Kambisseril property and the Pulinilkunnathil property, constructing a house at Kambisseril where his elder son Chacko's family also lived. He later purchased the Vettatheri paddy field at Karipuzha and a vast paddy field at Vellore, Puthiyakavu. He became a wealthy and influential man in Mavelikara. This settlement was established in AD 1806.

The Mavelikara Branch — Ulahannan Ittyavirah
5th Generation · Rev. Peet · CMS Church · Founding Co-Father
▸

Itty Avirah — who settled at Kattachira — was the brother of Mr. Vazhappallil Varghese, the founder of the Powathikunnel–Palakkamannil families at Mallappally. This makes the two founding fathers of the Mavelikara family not merely associates, but members of the same family. Itty Avirah married from the Vellappallil family of Pallom. His descendant in the fourth generation, Mr. Ittyavirah Ulahannan, moved to a place near Mallappally. Mr. Ulahannan Ittyavirah, his son and the fifth generation, studied Kalari at the Powathikunnel Kalari School. Tall and well-built, he was selected as the bodyguard and translator to the Anglican Missionary Rev. J. Peet of the Church Mission Society, who came to Kerala in 1833 and died at Mavelikara in 1865.

Anglican Missionaries from the Church Mission Society had been conducting Biblical work with the Malankara Suriyani Church from Kottayam. After disagreements at the famous Mavelikara Sunnahadose, both parties separated. The CMS subsequently built their own churches and schools. When Rev. Peet moved to Mavelikara, Ulahannan Ittyavirah followed and served him for twenty-seven years as his Bodyguard, Translator, and Executive Officer.

Through his service, the construction of the CSI Church (originally CMS) at Mavelikara, the Bishop Hodges School, and several other church buildings were carried out. Rev. Peet built a house for Mr. Ittyavirah at the Thattassery Land. His close relationship with the missionary attracted enmity from some quarters, led by Rev. Tharyan of the CMS Church. After Rev. Peet's death, they attempted to drive Ittyavirah away from Mavelikara — but Powathikunnel family members from Mallappally, together with the sons of Mallappally Varghese, stood with him, and the opposition finally made peace in 1814.

Mr. Ulahannan Ittyavirah married Smt. Elikutty of Vallapurackal House, Kottayam. They had six sons and two daughters. He died at the age of 92 at Puthuparampil House, and both he and his wife were laid to rest at the CMS Church, Praikkara.

Mallappally Varghese of Powathikunnel House and Ulahannan Ittyavirah are together considered the founding fathers of the Mavelikara unit of the Powathikunnel Kudumbayogam. This family directory covers their descendants.

✍ About Our Family Name

▾
Canonical House Name
Powathikunnel
Historical Variants
Powathikunnil  ·  Povathikunnel  ·  Povathikunnil  ·  Pavothikunnel  ·  Pavothikunnil

Historical records, church registers and civil documents may use any of these spellings. Throughout this website, Powathikunnel is adopted as the canonical family house name.

Powathikunnel, Powathikunnil, Pavothikunnel are likely variant spellings/transliterations of the same Malayalam family or house name. In Kerala, many family names are derived from a tharavadu (ancestral house) name, and the ending can be transliterated into English in different ways: -kunnel often comes from Malayalam കുന്നേൽ (kunnel), meaning "at/on the hill" or "hill-side", while -kunnil often comes from കുന്നിൽ (kunnil), which literally means "on the hill."

Because Malayalam sounds do not always map neatly to English spelling, the same family name may appear in official documents as Powathikunnel, Powathikunnil, Povathikunnel, or Povathikunnil. Whether they refer to the same family depends on the original Malayalam spelling and local family usage. In many cases, these English spellings are treated as equivalent, but for legal documents (passport, visa, certificates, etc.), the exact spelling on official records should be used consistently.

Since the family is from Mavelikara, Powathikunnel and Powathikunnil are very likely alternative English spellings of the same ancestral house name rather than completely different names. In central Kerala, especially in areas around Mavelikara, house names ending in -kunnel (കുന്നേൽ) and -kunnil (കുന്നിൽ) can become mixed when written in English because Malayalam pronunciation varies slightly by locality and family tradition. The original Malayalam spelling is what determines whether they are technically the same name.

For example, Powathikunnil would typically correspond to പൊവാത്തിക്കുന്നിൽ (or a similar Malayalam spelling), while Powathikunnel would typically correspond to പൊവാത്തിക്കുന്നേൽ. These are closely related forms, and members of the same extended family sometimes use different English spellings across generations or documents.

Chitramezhuthu K. M. Varghese
8th Generation · Mavelikara Powathikunnel

Our forefather Chitramezhuthu K. M. Varghese was the first person to systematically record the history of the Mavelikara Powathikunnel families, documenting nearly two hundred years of the family's life at Mavelikara.

His research extended to Palayoor village, Chavakkadu — tracing its history across more than 1,600 years — establishing the family's origins in the Pakalomattom Tharavadu of Palayoor among the earliest Christian converts of Kerala following the arrival of St. Thomas in AD 52.

He is rightly regarded as the First Historian of the Mavelikara Powathikunnel Families. Everything recorded here stands on the foundation he laid.

✏ Editor's Note

This family directory is compiled as part of an ongoing effort to digitize, document and preserve the complete history of the Powathikunnel family for future generations.

This family tree is the continuation of a journey that began long before the digital age. The foundation was laid by my grandfather, whose dedication to recording our family's history preserved valuable stories, relationships, and memories for future generations. His work was later expanded and enriched by my uncle Thomas George (Roy) and a team of family members who collaborated to produce the family history book from which much of this information has been drawn.

The directory currently covers 775 members across 12 generations from two family trees — the Mallappally Varghese tree and the Ulahannan Ittyavirah tree. Data has been sourced from the Powathikunnel Kudumbayogam family history books (Mavelikara Powathikunnel Family — 2007) and family records.

What makes this family tree different from many traditional genealogies is the principle on which it is now re-built. Historically, Christian family records in Kerala often followed a patriarchal model, tracing primarily the male lineage and giving less prominence to daughters and their descendants. This digitized tree takes a different approach. It is based on kinship through shared ancestry and bloodline rather than gender. Every descendant, whether through a son or a daughter, is considered an equally important part of our family story.

This is not merely a record of names and dates; it is a celebration of our shared heritage and the connections that unite us. My hope is that by preserving and expanding this work, we honour those who came before us while creating a lasting legacy for those who will come after us.

Jacob Oommen (Legy)
Generation 10  ·  2026

🏠 House Names: A Unique Heritage of Kerala

One of the distinctive features of Kerala’s social and cultural history is the tradition of the house name (Veettuperu). Among Kerala Christians, a person’s identity has traditionally been linked not only to their given name but also to their ancestral home. Long before modern street addresses and house numbers, families were identified by the name of their house or homestead. Church records, marriage registers, land deeds, and local histories frequently referred to individuals by their house names, making them an invaluable resource for genealogy.

The house name is often passed down through generations and remains a lasting symbol of a family’s history and identity, even after descendants have moved away from the ancestral home. It serves as a unique link between present-day generations and their roots, preserving a sense of belonging and continuity across time.

Although similar traditions exist in other parts of the world, Kerala’s practice is particularly distinctive. In Norway and Sweden, families were once identified by the names of their farms, many of which later became permanent surnames. In Iceland, farms still have names that help identify families and their ancestral lands. These traditions, like Kerala’s, reflect a deep connection between people, their homes, and their heritage.

What makes Kerala’s tradition especially remarkable is that the house name often predates modern surnames, is inherited across many generations, appears in church and civil records, and continues to identify descendants long after the original ancestral home has disappeared. For genealogists, a Kerala house name is often more valuable than a surname, providing a unique key to tracing family origins and preserving the story of each family for future generations.

Powathikunnel is itself such a house name — the thread that binds every member of this directory across ten generations.

ⓘ Guidelines for Uploading Vintage Photographs

1. Original photographs only. Only original Black & White, Sepia-toned, or Vintage photographs may be uploaded. Please do not upload modern colour photographs that have been converted to black & white or sepia using filters.

2. Group photographs are encouraged. Group photographs are especially welcome, as they help identify family members and preserve shared memories.

3. Add basic information. Please include the Title, the Date (or the closest estimated year), and the Location (if known).

4. Identify the people. Please name those in the photograph, separating each name with a comma (,).

5. Include maiden names. For married women, please include their maiden (birth) name where known. Example: Saramma (Varghese) Oommen.

A final note: every photograph tells a story. Even if you know only a few details, please upload it. Someone else in the family may be able to identify the people, date the occasion, or complete the story. Together, we can preserve our family's history for generations to come.
